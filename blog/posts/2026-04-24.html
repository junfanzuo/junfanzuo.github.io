1. Vowel Gradation

1.1 Overview of Vowel Gradation

In Sanskrit, many verb and noun stems show an internal alternation of the vowel in the root. This is vowel gradation (or ablaut) which has three distinct grades. The zero grade is the basic form of the root with no added vowel; the full grade (guṇa) is created by inserting a short a; and the lengthened grade (vṛddhi) inserts a long ā. These modifications do not themselves change the meaning of the root, they rather belong to the morphological system. For example, the root √भृ (bhṛ) appears in the zero grade as √भृ, in guṇa as √भर (bhar), and in vṛddhi as भार (bhār).

Because the zero grade is defined by the absence of -a-, there is no row for a in the list of simple vowels. When a root’s vowel is a it only appears when the stem is in guṇa or vṛddhi. 1.2 Vowel Gradation and Verb Classes

Sanskrit verbs are divided into classes based on how they form their present tense stems. In Class I the present stem is built by placing the root in guṇa and then adding a final -a; thus √भृ becomes भर, yielding भरति (bharati). Class IV and Class VI keep the root in the zero grade and add -ya- or -a- respectively. Class X uses the suffix -aya- and may take the root in zero grade, guṇa or vṛddhi depending on what produces a heavy syllable. Many class X verbs are denominal, which are formed from nouns by removing the final vowel and adding -aya- along with the appropriate personal endings. Examples include cint → चिन्तयति, kath → कथयति, and cur → चोरयति. Some verbs whose roots end in a vowel use vṛddhi in this context to produce the desired weight.

1.3 Causatives and Heavy Syllables

The same process of strengthening the root and adding -aya- can also form causative verbs. Causatives express “to make someone do X.” For example, the root विश् (viś) becomes वेशयति (veśayati), दृष् (dṛś) becomes दर्शयति, and √भृ becomes भारयति. Roots ending in -ā insert -p- before -aya-, as in स्था → स्थापयति. Unlike the regular verb classes, causatives exhibit a real semantic shift: the morphological change signals that the action is caused rather than simply performed.

1.4 Roots Ending in a Vowel

When a root ends in i/ī or u/ū, the usual guṇa forms e and o do not appear. The historical development was that the diphthongs *ai and *au changed into e and o only when followed by a consonant; before another vowel, they remained as ay or av and the glide -y- or -v- appears. Thus नयति (nayati) comes from the root √नी (nī), द्रवति (dravati) from √द्रु (dru), and भवति (bhavati) from √भू (bhū). We can summarise this historical rule: original *ai/*au become e/o before consonants, but remain ay/av (or āy/āv in vṛddhi) before vowels.

1.5 Roots Listed in Guṇa

Most dictionaries list verbal roots in their zero grade. From this basic form one can add -a- to obtain guṇa and -ā- to obtain vṛddhi. However, not all roots follow the regular pattern of adding a/ā before the root vowel. Some roots add the strengthening vowel after the original vowel; for these it is impossible to reconstruct the zero grade by inspection, so the root is conventionally cited in guṇa. An example is वद (vad), whose zero grade is उद (ud) and vṛddhi form is वाद (vād). Other examples include वच (zero grade उच / vṛddhi वाच), स्वप (sup / स्वाप), यज (ij / याज) and रक्ष् (ṛkṣ / राक्ष्). Because the zero grade of such verbs is difficult to recognise—vocalic y and v become i and u before consonants—it is easier to memorise them as listed.

Roots ending in a nasal are also customarily cited in guṇa. The zero grades of गम् (gam) and मन् (man) would technically be gm and mn, but these forms are almost never used. When a consonant follows, the nasal changes to -a-, yielding ga and ma; from these one could not reconstruct the correct guṇa or vṛddhi forms (gam / gām, man / mān). Hence such roots are listed in their full grade to reveal the underlying pattern.
