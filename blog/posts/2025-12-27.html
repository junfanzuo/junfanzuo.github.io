I am currently restudying logic. This post serves as the review notes for it. I use forall x (UBC edition): An Introduction to Formal Logic by Jonathan Ichikawa and P. D. Magnus as the textbook. A useful website to study Logic (or other wonderful philosophy) is Critical Thinking Web developed by Joe Lau at HKU.

§0.1 Argument

I shall begin this chapter with a goofy example. My girlfriend (let’s call her Ann1) is mad at me because I sometimes don’t answer her phone – I am playing video games and IT CANNOT BE PAUSED. Ann then accused me of not loving her anymore. This is not a healthy conversation in a relationship but Ann’s argument is:

Premise 1 If I don’t answer the phone, then I don’t love Ann anymore.

Premise 2 I don’t answer the phone.

Conclusion Therefore, I don’t love Ann anymore.

An argument includes Premises and Conclusions. In Ann’s argument, the Premises are the first two lines and the Conclusion is the last sentence. Ann’s Conclusion is supported by her Premises so we have the definition for them: Conclusion is the proposition you want to believe or argue for and Premise is the reason to support your Conclusion.

§0.2 Validity & Soundness

(I realized that I’ve spent too much on the previous chapter. I don’t want to make this post too basic since my goal is to learn something new. I’ll skip contents that I’ve already known, and they are marked with §0.x.)

Ann’s argument is valid2. Let’s take a look at Ichikawa and Magnus’s definition of Validity: “An argument is deductively valid if and only if it is impossible3 for all the premises to be true while the conclusion is false” (2019, p. 15). Clearly the conclusion in Ann’s argument is true if Premise 1 and 2 are true (modus ponens) but this is intuitively disturbing – I get mad as well when I hear something like this because Ann is just being goofy! Logic in fact only cares about the structure not the content. Now two important cases: (a) Tautological Conclusion (i.e., any argument with a tautological conclusion is valid), and (b) Inconsistent Premises (i.e., any argument with logically inconsistent premises is valid).

There is a particularly confusing argument on Critical Thinking Web:

Premise 1 All cows are mammals.

Conclusion Therefore, Therefore, the sun is larger than the moon.

From the previous case (a), this argument seems to be valid because it has a tautological conclusion – the sun is indeed larger than the moon, obviously! However, this is not a tautology thus case (a) does not fit here. If you read my footnote, I distinguished impossible and logically impossible. Consider the sun and the moon. It is not impossible that the sun must be larger than the moon because the sun could have shrunk and be smaller than the moon. What is logically impossible is that the sun is larger than the moon and the sun is smaller than the moon (contradiction). Therefore, the conclusion in the above argument could be false so the argument is invalid.

Another concept is Soundness. A sound argument iff it is valid and all the premises are true.
