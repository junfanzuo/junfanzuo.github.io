1. Prepositions & Preverbs in Sanskrit

We have prepositions in English such as to, from, with, without, towards, and around. Because Sanskrit nouns already change their endings according to case, Sanskrit does not need as many independent prepositions as English does. Very often, the case ending itself tells us the function of the noun. However, Sanskrit still has some small words that behave somewhat like prepositions. More importantly, Sanskrit often places similar small elements before verbs. These are called preverbs.

2. Prepositions Are Usually Postpositions

In English, a preposition normally comes before the noun: towards the city, with Kal'tsit, without anger

Sanskrit often places such a word after the noun. Strictly speaking, these words are therefore better called postpositions. Nevertheless, many textbooks still use the familiar word “preposition” because the function is similar to English prepositions. Consider the following examples:

2.1. प्रति prati — towards

प्रति prati is used with ACC. It gives the idea of movement or direction towards something. 

राजा ग्रामं प्रति गच्छति rājā grāmaṃ prati gacchati: The king goes towards the village.

Notice that ग्रामम् grāmam (written ग्रामं here due to sandhi before प्रति) is ACC, and प्रति prati stands after it.

2.2. सह saha — with

सह saha means “with” or “together with”. It is commonly used with INS. 

बालः मित्रेण सह क्रीडति bālaḥ mitreṇa saha krīḍati: The boy plays with a friend.

The noun मित्रेण mitreṇa is in INS, SG.

2.3. विना vinā — without

विना vinā means “without”. It can be used with INS, ACC or ABL. 

विद्यां विना जीवनं कठिनम् vidyāṃ vinā jīvanaṃ kaṭhinam: Life is difficult without knowledge.

3. Preverbs

A preverb is a short element placed before a verbal root or verbal form. When a preverb combines with a verb, the result is called a compound verb. Many Sanskrit preverbs originally express simple spatial ideas: towards, away, around, up, down, together, forth, and so on. When they combine with verbs, their meanings may become less literal, however.

3.1. Simple motion verbs often become more specific

When the basic verb has a very general meaning, especially a verb of motion, the preverb often gives a clearer direction. 

बालकः गच्छति bālakaḥ gacchati: The boy goes.
बालकः आगच्छति bālakaḥ āgacchati: The boy comes.

With आ ā-, the meaning becomes: The boy comes. The movement is now directed towards the speaker or towards a relevant place. 

बालकः निर्गच्छति bālakaḥ nirgacchati: The boy goes out.

With निर् nir-, the meaning becomes: The boy goes out. 

बालकः परिगच्छति bālakaḥ parigacchati: The boy goes around.

With परि pari-, the idea may be movement around something, or reaching/going about, depending on context.

3.2. Specific verbs may only change slightly

When the basic verb already has a precise meaning, the preverb may not produce a dramatic change. Sometimes it only adds a little change. Sometimes it creates an idiomatic meaning that must be learned separately. 

नरः शृणोति naraḥ śṛṇoti: The man hears/listens.
नरः निशृणोति naraḥ niśṛṇoti: The man listens carefully.

Depending on context, this may suggest listening closely, listening downwards, or listening with attention. The preverb does not always translate neatly into a separate English word.

3.3. Do Not Translate Preverbs Mechanically

One of the most common mistakes is to translate every preverb as if it had one fixed English equivalent. This does not work well. A preverb has a general direction or flavour, but the final meaning depends on the verb it joins.

For example, सम् sam- often suggests “together”. With a motion verb, this may be very clear. 

जनाः संगच्छन्ते janāḥ saṃgacchante: The people come together.

But with other verbs, सम् sam- may suggest completeness, intensity, harmony, or a more idiomatic meaning. 

कविः श्लोकं सम्लिखति kaviḥ ślokaṃ saṃlikhati: The poet writes the verse fully/completely.

This may be understood differently. The idea of “together” is no longer a simple physical direction.

3.4. A Verb May Have More Than One Preverb

Sanskrit can stack preverbs. A compound verb may contain two preverbs, and occasionally even three. The meaning is not always the simple sum of the parts, but recognising the pieces helps us analyse the form. 

राजा उपागच्छति rājā upāgacchati: The king approaches.

Here उप upa- and आ ā- combine with गम् gam, “to go”. 

सैनिकाः पर्यागच्छन्ति sainikāḥ paryāgacchanti: The soldiers come/arrive around.

3.5. Absolutives of Compound Verbs

Compound verbs also affect the formation of absolutives. Simple verbs often use one type of absolutive ending, but compound verbs usually use -ya or -tya.

The practical point is this: when a verb has a preverb, expect its absolutive form to look different from the absolutive of the simple verb. 

राजा नगरं गत्वा विश्राम्यति rājā nagaraṃ gatvā viśrāmyati: Having gone to the city, the king rests.

Here गत्वा gatvā is the absolutive of the simple verb गम् gam, “to go”. 

राजा नगरम् आगत्य विश्राम्यति rājā nagaram āgatya viśrāmyati: Having come to the city, the king rests.

Because the verb is compound, आ + गम् ā + gam, the absolutive is आगत्य āgatya.
