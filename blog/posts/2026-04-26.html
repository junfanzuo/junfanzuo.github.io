1. Absolutives

In English, we often describe several actions by using finite verbs: “Kal'tsit opened the book, read the verse, and wrote a note.” Sanskrit often prefers a different structure. A Sanskrit sentence may have only one main finite verb, while the other actions are expressed through non-finite verbal forms. This means that Sanskrit frequently says something like: “Having opened the book and having read the verse, Kal'tsit wrote a note.” The final action may be expressed by a normal finite verb, while the earlier or connected actions are expressed by forms such as absolutives or participles.

An absolutive is an indeclinable verbal form. It usually means “having done X”. It is used when one action is completed before another action. In many grammars, it is also called a gerund, but “absolutive” is the term I will use here.

1.1 Basics of Absolutives

If the root means “to read,” the absolutive means “having read.” It usually gives background action before the main verb.

Consider the following example:

शिष्यः ग्रन्थं पठित्वा लिखति। śiṣyaḥ granthaṃ paṭhitvā likhati.
“Having read the book, the student writes.”

Here, पठित्वा means “having read.” The main finite verb is लिखति. The reading happens first, and the writing follows.

1.2 Formation of the Absolutive

For simple verbs, the absolutive is usually formed with: verbal root + त्वा (-tvā). However, Sanskrit roots do not always combine with suffixes mechanically. Sometimes an extra -i- appears before -tvā. This does not change the meaning. It is simply part of the form that must be recognised. Consider the following examples:

पठ् “to read” → पठित्वा “having read”
हस् “to laugh” → हसित्वा “having laughed”

बालः जलं पीत्वा क्रीडति। bālaḥ jalaṃ pītvā krīḍati.
“Having drunk water, the boy plays.”

1.3 The Absolutive Has No Case Ending

A very important feature of the absolutive is that it is indeclinable. This means that it does not change according to gender, number, or case. A noun may be MASC, FEM, NTR, SG, DU, or PL; an absolutive does not care. It remains the same. Consider the following examples:

शिष्यः ग्रन्थं पठित्वा लिखति।
“Having read the book, the student writes.”

शिष्यौ ग्रन्थं पठित्वा लिखतः।
“Having read the book, the two students write.”

शिष्याः ग्रन्थं पठित्वा लिखन्ति।
“Having read the book, the students write.”

The subject changes from singular to dual to plural, but पठित्वा remains unchanged.

1.4 The Subject of the Absolutive

The action expressed by the absolutive normally belongs to the same agent as the main verb. In other words, the person who does the absolutive action is usually the same person who performs the main action. Consider the following example:

कन्या पुष्पं दृष्ट्वा हसति। kanyā puṣpaṃ dṛṣṭvā hasati.
“Having seen the flower, the girl smiles.”

In this case, the girl is the one who sees the flower, and the girl is also the one who smiles. We do not need to repeat the subject inside the absolutive.

2. ta-Participle

A ta-participle is a verbal adjective formed with the suffix -ta-. It is called a participle because it is made from a verb, but it behaves like an adjective. Its basic meaning is often “having been X-ed”.

2.1 Basics of ta-Participle

The basic pattern is: verbal root + त (-ta-). After this, normal adjective endings are added. Consider the following examples:

लिख् “to write” → लिखित “having been written”
पूज् “to worship” → पूजित “having been worshipped”

2.2 Passive Meaning

When the verb can naturally be passive, the ta-participle usually has a passive meaning. Consider the following example:

लिखितं पत्रं पठामि। likhitaṃ patraṃ paṭhāmi.
“I read the written letter.”
Or: “I read the letter that has been written.”

Here, लिखितम् describes पत्रम्. The letter did not write something; rather, it was written by someone. Therefore the meaning is passive.

2.3 Active Meaning with Intransitive Verbs

Some verbs cannot sensibly be made passive. For example, verbs meaning “to come,” “to stand,” “to sit,” or “to become” often describe what the subject itself does or experiences. In such cases, the ta-participle may have an active meaning. Consider the following example:

शिष्यः आगतः। śiṣyaḥ āgataḥ.
“The student has come.”

In this case, आगतः does not mean “having been come,” which would make no sense. It means “come” or “having come.”

2.4 The ta-Participle Must Agree with a Noun

Unlike the absolutive, the ta-participle is not indeclinable. It behaves like an adjective and must agree with the noun it describes in case, number, and gender. The participle changes because the noun changes. Consider the following examples:

लिखितः ग्रन्थः likhitaḥ granthaḥ
“the written book” — MASC, NOM, SG

लिखितं पत्रम् likhitaṃ patram
“the written letter” — NTR, NOM/ACC, SG

लिखिता कथा likhitā kathā
“the written story” — FEM, NOM, SG

3. Infinitive

The Sanskrit infinitive usually corresponds to English expressions such as “to read,” “to eat,” “to remember,” or “to write.”

3.1 Basics of Infinitive

The infinitive is commonly formed with: guṇa-grade root + तुम् (-tum). In practice, some roots insert an extra -i- before -tum, while others undergo sandhi before the t. Therefore, infinitives must often be learned together with the root. Consider the following examples:

पठ् “read” → पठितुम् “to read”
लिख् “write” → लेखितुम् “to write”
स्मृ “remember” → स्मर्तुम् “to remember”
पच् “cook” → पक्तुम् “to cook”

3.2 Use of the Infinitive

Infinitives are often used after verbs meaning “to want,” “to intend,” “to be able,” “to begin,” or “to go for the purpose of doing something.” Consider the following example:

छात्रः संस्कृतं पठितुम् इच्छति। chātraḥ saṃskṛtaṃ paṭhitum icchati.
“The student wants to study Sanskrit.”

Here, इच्छति is the finite verb, “wants.” पठितुम् is the infinitive, “to study” or “to read.”

3.3 Infinitives of Class X and Causative Verbs

Verbs of Class X and causative verbs commonly form their infinitives with -ayitum. This comes from the present stem without its final -a. Consider the following examples:

कथयति “he tells” → कथयितुम् “to tell”
दर्शयति “he shows” → दर्शयितुम् “to show”
