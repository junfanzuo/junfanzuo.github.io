1. Verbs & Present Tense

1.1. What is the potential mood?

Moods express the speaker's attitude toward the action described by the verb. There are three moods in Sanskrit: indicative, imperative, and potential. Potential mood is used to express possibility or hypothetical situations (e.g., "I might go to the store"), while indicative and imperative moods express factual statements and commands, respectively.

1.2. What is a verbal root? How is it different from a verbal stem?

A verbal root is the basic form of a verb from which all other forms are derived. A verbal stem is a form of the verb that has been modified by adding affixes or other grammatical elements.

1.3. How is the present tense (PRES) stem of a verb formed?

There are ten different ways to form the present tense stem of a verb in Sanskrit, depending on the verb class. For Class I, the root is modified by adding -a- before its root vowel and then -a- is added to the end. For Class IV, -ya- is added to the end of the root. For Class VI, we either add -a- at the end of the root, or we add a nasal before the final consonant and then add -a- after it. After the stem is formed, we add the following endings at the end of the stem to form PRES:

Sg Du Pl
1st -mi -vaḥ -maḥ
2nd -si -thaḥ -tha
3rd -ti -taḥ -nti

1.4. What are 'finite' verb forms?

Finite verb forms are those that have a specific person, number, and tense. They are contrasted with non-finite verb forms, which serve as verbal nouns, adjectives, or participles. Consider:

Having read the book, Kal'tsit laughs.

In this case, the verb "laughs" is finite because it has a specific person (3rd) and number (Sg), while the verb "having read" is non-finite.

1.5. How does Sanskrit express a continuous present?

There is no 'continuous' present in Sanskrit. Instead, it uses the present tense to express ongoing actions. Consider:

बालकः पठति bālakaḥ paṭhati

In this case, we can either translate it as "The boy is reading" or "The boy reads."

1.6. What is the difference in meaning between the different verb classes?

There is generally no difference in meaning between the different verb classes. The 10 verb classes are purely structural and morphological. They exist simply to tell you how to conjugate the verb, not what the verb means.

2. Nominals

2.1. What kinds of nominal forms are there?

Nominals include nouns, pronouns, participles and adjectives.

2.2. Which three grammatical categories are Sanskrit nouns marked for?

Nominals are marked for case, number, and gender. There are eight cases in Sanskrit, and they mark the role of the noun in the sentence:

Nominative (NOM): the subject of a sentence and for anything referring to the subject.
Vocative (VOC): used to address someone directly. (i.e., O ...!)
Accusative (ACC): the direct object of a verb or the goal of a motion.
Instrumental (INS): used to indicate the means by which an action is performed (i.e., with ...). INS can also be used for the performer of something that is done (i.e., by ...). Moreover, INS can refer to people who are accompanying the performer of the action (i.e., with ...).
Dative (DAT): used to indicate the purpose of an action (i.e., for ...) or the recipient of an action (i.e., to ...).
Ablative (ABL): used to indicate the source or origin of something (i.e., from ...; of ...).
Genitive (GEN): used to indicate possession or a relationship of belonging (i.e., of ...; for/to ...).
Locative (LOC): used to indicate location or time where something is (i.e., in ...; on ...; at ...; into...).

Sanskrit has three grammatical numbers: Singular (Sg), Dual (Du), and Plural (Pl). There are three grammatical genders as well: Masculine (MASC), Feminine (FEM), and Neuter (NTR).

2.3. What does the grammatical term 'agreement' mean?

Agreement refers to the matching of grammatical categories between different words in a sentence. That is, a noun and its adjective must agree in case, number, and gender. Consider:

नवः अश्वः navaḥ (MASC.NOM.SG) aśvaḥ (NOM.SG): the new horse
नवस्य अश्वस्य navasya (MASC.GEN.SG) aśvasya (GEN.SG): of the new horse
नवानाम् अश्वानाम् navānām (MASC.GEN.PL) aśvānām (GEN.PL): of the new horses
