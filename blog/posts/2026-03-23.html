Logic III: Quantificational Logic

23 Mar 2026

A. Translation

Quantificational Logic (QL) retains all the truth-functional connectives of Propositional Logic. Two quantifiers are introduced here: The Universal Quantifier (∀) and The Existential Quantifier (∃). There are Predicates which assign properties and relations as well. Details of the grammar and syntax of QL will not be explained here but readers can refer to Harry Gensler's Introduction to Logic for an easy start. This section will merely present some translations of QL.

Wk = Kal'tsit is wise.
Lkw = Kal'tsit loves Warfarin.
~∀xWx = It is not the case that, for all x, x is wise. (or, Not every one is wise.)
~∃x(Wx & ~Px) = No one is both wise and not pretty.

B. Interpretations

A Universe of Discourse (UD) defines the a domain of things that we are talking about. Consider:

Everyone is satisfied.

In this example, we tend to believe that "everyone" here refers to a certain group of people only - we are not talking about people in another country or people who are dead because that would be semantically strange. Normally what we are referring to is the people in this room. A UD allows us to apply the quantifiers for a certain group of elements. Consider: 

UD = {Kal'tsit, Warfarin}

Given this UD, ∀x refers to Kal'tsit AND Warfarin, and ∃x refers to Kal'tsit OR Warfarin.

We can assign extensions to predicate. Consider:

Extension(Wise) = {Kal'tsit}
Extension(Dumb) = {Warfarin}
Extension(Friendly) = {Kal'tsit, Warfarin}

∃xWx is true because there is at least one element in the UD that is true for Extension(Wise) (i.e., Wk is true). ∀xDx is false because Dk is false. ∀x(Wx v Fx) is true because (Wk v Fk) and (Ww v Fw) are both true.

Consider the previous UD again. The following are examples of quantidied sentences with polyadic predicates.

∀xLxx = Kal'tsit loves Kal'tsit, and Warfarin loves Warfarin.
∃xLkx = Kal'tsit loves Kal'tsit, or Kal'tsit loves Warfarin.
∃x∀yLxy = Either Kal'tsit loves everyone (both herself and Warfarin), or Warfarin loves everyone (both herself and Kal'tsit).

C. Proofs for QL

Logic II introduced Natural Deduction rules for Propositional Logic (PL). The rules applied for PL can be used for QL as well. Consider:

1. ∀xPx ∨ ∃yQy
2. ¬∀xPx & Ra
3. ¬∀xPx          &E 2
4. ∃yQy           ∨E 1, 3
5. ∃yQy ∨ ∀zSz    ∨I 4

In addition to the rules for PL, there are new rules for QL. Consider:

Universal Elimination 
m. ∀χΦ
n. Φ [χ/c]        ∀E m
Existential Introduction 
m. Φ
n. ∃χΦ [[c/χ]]    ∃I m
Universal Introduction 
m. Φ
n. ∀χΦ [c1/χ]
Existential Elimination 
m. ∃χΦ
n. Φ [χ/c2]               
o. Ψ
p. Ψ
Quantifier Negation 
∀xPx = ~∃x~Px
∃xPx = ~∀x~Px

Below is an exercise question for this post, extracted from Gensler (2017, p. 276).

Translate into logic, and say whether it is valid (and give a proof) or invalid (and give a refutation).
Anything is enjoyable is either immoral or fattening.
Therefore, if nothing is immoral, then everything that is not fattening is not enjoyable.

Consider the solution:

1.  ∀x(Ex → (Ix v Fx))
2.  | ~(~∃xIx → ∀x(~Fx → ~Ex))          (for reductio)
3.  | ~∃xIx                             from 2
4.  | ~∀x(~Fx → ~Ex)                    from 2
5.  | ∀x~Ix                             QN 3
6.  | ∃x~(~Fx → ~Ex)                    QN 4
7.  | ~(~Fa → ~Ea)                      from 6
8.  | ~Fa                               from 7
9.  | Ea                                from 7
10. | Ea → (Ia v Fa)                    ∀E 1
11. | Ia v Fa                           →E 9, 10
12. | Ia                                DS 8, 11
13. | ~Ia                               ∀E 5
14. ~∃xIx → ∀x(~Fx → ~Ex)               ~I 2-13
